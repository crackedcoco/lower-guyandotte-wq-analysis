Background

What Are Water Quality Standards?

Water quality standards are legally enforceable limits that protect our rivers, streams, and drinking water sources. They define the maximum allowable concentrations of pollutants that waterways can contain while still supporting their designated usesswimming, fishing, drinking water supply, and aquatic life.

In West Virginia, these standards are established under WV 47CSR2 (Water Quality Standards) and enforced by the DEP. When monitoring data shows that a stream exceeds these limits, it becomes "impaired" and subject to cleanup requirements under the federal Clean Water Act.

Why does this matter? The Lower Guyandotte watershed has been heavily impacted by coal mining, resulting in elevated conductivity, selenium, and other pollutants. Understanding which streams violate standardsand by how muchis critical for prioritizing restoration efforts and holding polluters accountable.

Technical Details

Methodology

WV 47CSR2 Standards

Analysis applies West Virginia-specific water quality criteria rather than generic EPA standards. Key differences include Fe: 1.5 mg/L, pH: 6.0-9.0, and Al Dissolved: 0.75 mg/L.

Ambient vs Point Source

Data filtered to exclude point source discharge monitoring. Ambient stream samples identified by AU_ID patterns not containing "SOURCE" designation.

Non-Detect Handling

Left-censored data processed using Kaplan-Meier survival analysis for summary statistics. Half-detection-limit substitution used for trend analysis.

Fecal Coliform Protocol

Two-part standard assessment: 200 CFU/100mL geometric mean calculated per station, plus 400 CFU/100mL single-sample maximum threshold.

Trend Analysis Methodology

Statistical Model: Non-parametric Mann-Kendall test for monotonic trends, chosen because it doesn't assume normal distributionideal for environmental data with seasonal patterns and outliers.

Slope Estimation: Sen's slope estimator calculates the median of all pairwise slopes between data points, providing a robust measure of trend magnitude resistant to outliers.

Confidence Level: Significance threshold p < 0.05 (95% confidence). Trends reported only when we're 95% confident they're not due to random chance.

Key Assumptions: Data points are temporally independent; seasonal adjustments applied where appropriate; minimum 10 data points required per parameter.

Note: Trend percentages represent relative change from earliest to most recent decade median values.

Hotspot Scoring

Composite impairment score per HUC12 based on parameter-specific violation counts weighted by ecological severity.

Conductivity Benchmark (300 S/cm)

Source: U.S. EPA (2011). A Field-Based Aquatic Life Benchmark For Conductivity In Central Appalachian Streams. EPA/600/R-10/023F.

What it means: The 300 S/cm threshold represents the conductivity level at which 5% of macroinvertebrate genera experience extirpation (local extinction) from streams. This benchmark was derived from field data in Central Appalachian streams, including West Virginia.

Important note: West Virginia has not adopted a numeric conductivity standard. The 300 S/cm benchmark is EPA guidance, not a state-enforceable limit. However, it remains the best available science for assessing ionic toxicity impacts on aquatic life in this region.
